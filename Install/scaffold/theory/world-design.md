# World Design

> **Authority:** Rank 11 — Advisory only
> **Layer:** Theory

Principles for building game worlds — how to create believable, interesting, and cohesive environments that serve gameplay.

---

## Sense of Place

- Every location should feel like it exists for a reason beyond gameplay. A village needs a water source. A fortress needs sight lines to the approach. A market needs a crossroads. When the player can intuit why a place is where it is, the world feels coherent without explanation.
- Architecture, vegetation, weather, and decay should tell a story of who lives (or lived) here. A well-maintained garden implies an active caretaker. Crumbling walls imply abandonment or conflict. These details register subconsciously even when the player is not actively reading the environment.
- Consistent internal logic matters more than realism. A floating city held aloft by magic is believable if the game establishes how that magic works and applies it consistently. A normal city with doors that open the wrong way breaks immersion because it violates expectations without explanation.
- A place that feels lived-in is more memorable than one that is merely beautiful. Wear patterns on floors, soot stains above fireplaces, paths worn through grass — signs of habitation make spaces feel real. Pristine environments feel like stage sets.
- Cultural identity should be readable from architecture and layout alone. If you removed all text and NPCs, the player should still be able to distinguish a militaristic society's settlement from a mercantile one based on building shapes, road layouts, and material choices.

## World Rules & Internal Logic

- Establish what is possible and impossible early. If the player learns that magic exists in the first hour, they will accept magical solutions to problems. If magic appears for the first time in hour twenty to solve a plot problem, it feels like a cheat.
- Players accept any rules if they are consistent. Gravity works sideways, water flows uphill, the dead walk — all fine, as long as the game never contradicts its own established logic without acknowledging the contradiction.
- Breaking your own rules without explanation destroys trust faster than any bug. A locked door in a world where the player can destroy walls. A waist-high fence that blocks a character who can jump buildings. These inconsistencies teach the player that the world is arbitrary, and an arbitrary world is not worth exploring.
- Magic systems, technology levels, and physics should follow their own internal logic even if they differ from reality. Define the constraints, the costs, and the limits. A fireball spell that requires fuel and a spark is more believable than one that simply exists because the designer wanted ranged attacks.
- When you must break a rule for gameplay reasons, find a diegetic justification. A barrier the player cannot cross is more acceptable as a magical ward with a visible effect than as an invisible wall. The justification does not need to be elaborate — it just needs to exist.

## Biome & Region Design

- Each region should be visually, mechanically, and thematically distinct. Visual distinction means the player can identify the region from a screenshot. Mechanical distinction means the region changes how they play. Thematic distinction means the region communicates something different about the world.
- Transitions between regions should feel gradual, not jarring. A forest does not end at a line and become desert. Trees thin out, grass becomes sparse, soil becomes sandy. Transition zones give the player time to adjust expectations and notice the shift.
- Biomes should influence gameplay. Ice means slippery footing or cold mechanics. Desert means heat management or limited water. Dense forest means limited visibility and close-range encounters. A biome that only changes the skybox and ground texture is a missed opportunity.
- Avoid palette-swap biomes that only change color without changing feel. A blue forest and a red forest are the same forest. A dense canopy forest with filtered light and tangled undergrowth versus a sparse pine forest with long sight lines and rocky ground are meaningfully different experiences.
- Give each region at least one unique mechanic, resource, or interaction that cannot be found elsewhere. This creates reasons to travel and makes each region feel essential to the world rather than interchangeable.

## Scale & Density

- Bigger is not always better. A small world full of interesting things beats a large empty one every time. Players measure world quality by the density of meaningful interactions, not by the time it takes to walk across the map.
- Discovery density — how often the player finds something new — drives exploration satisfaction. If the player walks for five minutes without encountering anything interesting, they stop exploring and start fast-traveling. Maintain a steady rhythm of discoverable content.
- Pace landmark placement to maintain a sense of progress. The player should almost always be able to see their next potential destination from their current position. A horizon with nothing on it tells the player there is nothing worth walking toward.
- Negative space — intentionally empty areas — is valid for pacing but must be deliberate. A vast empty field before a city makes the city feel more impressive. An empty field between two empty fields feels like the designer ran out of ideas.
- Match the world's scale to the movement speed and traversal mechanics. A world designed for walking feels unbearably empty when the player gets a mount. A world designed for flying feels cluttered when the player is on foot. Scale and mobility must be designed together.
- Density should vary by region purpose. Hub areas and quest-dense zones should be packed. Wilderness connectors can be sparser. The contrast between dense and sparse areas creates rhythm.

## Verticality & Layering

- Vertical space adds depth without expanding the map footprint. A city with rooftops, streets, and sewers offers three times the exploration space of a flat city at the same horizontal scale.
- Underground, surface, and above-ground layers create exploration variety. Each layer should have its own visual identity, navigation rules, and encounter types. The underground feels different from the surface not just visually but mechanically.
- Height differences create natural vantage points and navigation puzzles. A cliff overlooking a valley gives the player information and a goal. Figuring out how to descend safely or climb up to a visible ledge is inherently engaging without any authored puzzle.
- Visible-from-above destinations create pull. When the player reaches a high point, the things they can see below become immediate goals. This is one of the most reliable exploration drivers in open-world design — let the player see before they go.
- Use vertical layering to create shortcuts and loops. A bridge that connects two previously separate areas, a zipline from a peak to a valley, an elevator that links underground and surface — these vertical connections reward exploration with efficient traversal.

## Lore Through Environment

- Architecture styles imply culture and history. Rounded arches suggest different values than sharp angles. Organic materials suggest different priorities than stone and metal. The player reads cultural identity from building shapes before they read a single word of lore.
- Ruins imply a past. Mixed architectural styles imply conflict or change — a temple converted into a fortress, a palace subdivided into tenements, foreign columns supporting a local roof. These details communicate centuries of history at a glance.
- Environmental details tell stories without words. Tools left beside an unfinished project. Wear patterns on a stone floor showing where generations walked. Graffiti over an official notice. A child's drawing in a soldier's quarters. These micro-narratives reward the observant player.
- Do not over-explain. Let players piece things together from fragments. A skeleton clutching a key near a locked door tells a complete story without a journal entry. Trust the player to read the environment — they are better at it than most designers expect.
- Contrast is your most powerful tool. A single flower growing through cracked pavement. A pristine room inside a ruined building. A child's toy in a battlefield. When the environment contradicts itself, the player notices and asks why — and that question is the beginning of engagement.

## Navigation & Wayfinding

- Landmarks should be visible from multiple locations and angles. A distinctive tower, a uniquely shaped mountain, a massive tree — these anchor the player's mental map. Without landmarks, every direction looks the same and orientation becomes impossible.
- Distinct skylines help orientation. The player should be able to look at the horizon and know roughly where they are based on what they can see. If every direction offers the same silhouette, the world lacks navigational structure.
- Natural paths — rivers, roads, ridgelines, valleys — guide without markers. Players follow paths instinctively. A river always leads somewhere. A road implies a destination. A ridgeline offers visibility. Use natural geography to create intuitive routes.
- Players should be able to form a mental map of the world after moderate play time. If they cannot, the world lacks spatial structure. Test this by asking a player to draw the world from memory — the result reveals whether the layout is legible or chaotic.
- Use lighting and color temperature to distinguish areas. Warm light in safe zones, cool light in dangerous ones. Saturated color in living areas, desaturated in dead ones. These subconscious cues supplement landmark-based navigation.

## Ecosystems & Inhabitants

- Worlds feel alive when they contain systems that exist independent of the player. NPCs with daily routines, animals that hunt and graze, weather that shifts — these systems communicate that the world was here before the player arrived and will continue after they leave.
- Ecosystems do not need to be complex to feel real. A predator that hunts prey, prey that flees, and scavengers that arrive after a kill is a three-part system that makes a forest feel like a functioning environment. Complexity should serve perception, not simulation accuracy.
- NPC behavior should be observable and consistent. A merchant who opens their shop in the morning and closes at night. Guards who patrol routes. Farmers who work fields. The player does not need to interact with these routines — simply seeing them exist builds world believability.
- The world should contain evidence of activity the player did not witness. Fresh tracks, recently extinguished campfires, harvested fields, repaired structures. These imply that life continues outside the player's view without requiring expensive always-on simulation.
- Do not simulate what the player cannot observe — it is wasted computation. An NPC's routine only matters when the player can see it or its effects. Offscreen NPCs can be state machines that update their observable state when the player arrives. The illusion of persistence is as effective as actual persistence and far cheaper.
